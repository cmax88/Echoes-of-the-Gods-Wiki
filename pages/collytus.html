Collytus



Description

Collytus is the Eastern municipality of Athena and is known for both its arts and agriculture. The upper portion of Collytus is dedicated to the theatrics of the city, hosting both the Olypieum and the Flavian Amphitheatre, while the mid and lower areas are reserved for nature and agriculture. This municipality is mainly for the common people of Athena and it is where workers come to laid their heads at night. Zeus became wildly outraged with his daughter’s leave from her lands and struck the Eastern lands down with a divine smite. From these smoldering grounds grew saintly fields that were always chalked full of crops. These fields feed all of Athena and bless the lands with their beauty. Zeus is now praised in his temple, the largest in the city, for his gift of life and prosperity to the mortals of Athena.

Lesser Locations



Fields of Divinity: The Fields of Divinity are situated in Collytus, the commoner district of Athena. This sprawling area of land is dotted with farms and fields, where the people of Collytus grow crops to feed their families and sell in the markets of Agora. The fields are irrigated by a network of canals and the work is hard but honest. Many of the crops are dedicated to the gods, and the residents of Collytus take pride in their offerings to the divine. Despite the long hours and demanding labor, the farmers of the fields are proud of their way of life and the connection they have to the land and their gods.


Flavian Amphitheatre: The Flavian Amphitheatre is a large, open-air arena with rows of tiered seating for spectators to enjoy games and contests. The stone walls are adorned with intricate carvings and sculptures, reflecting the gladiatorial battles that have taken place within its walls. Despite its rough exterior, the amphitheater remains a popular gathering place for the people of Collytus, providing entertainment and a sense of community.



Fort of Macedonia: The Fort of Macedonia stands tall in lower Collytus. Its rough, stone exterior is decorated with battlements and towers, signifying its purpose as a defensive structure. Despite its rough exterior, the fort exudes a sense of power and strength, symbolizing the might of the city and its army. Visitors can observe soldiers training in the courtyard, and the sounds of clashing weapons and shouting soldiers can be heard from the nearby streets. The Fort of Macedonia serves as a constant reminder of Athena's military might and its readiness to defend itself against any threats.



Gardens of the Eternal: The Gardens of the Eternal is a beautifully manicured park located in Collytus. Lush green grass stretches out in every direction, dotted with trees and shrubs that provide shade and beauty. Meandering pathways lead visitors through the garden, offering stunning views of fountains and sculptures dedicated to the gods. The park is a popular destination for locals seeking peace and respite from the hustle and bustle of daily life.



Olympieum: The Olympieum is a sprawling open area located in Northern Collytus. It is surrounded by lush tall trees, which creates a sense of seclusion. The track within is made of smooth, finely packed dirt, and is well-maintained. Spectators can sit on stone bleachers, cheering on their favorite athletes as they race around the track. The air is filled with the sounds of pounding feet and excited voices, creating a lively atmosphere for all who visit.



Pnyx: The Pynx is a large open-air forum located in the heart of Collytus. It is a place for public gatherings, speeches, and debates, surrounded by a tiered seating area for onlookers. The platform in the center of the Pynx is elevated, providing a clear view for all who attend. The area is well-maintained and often used for various events and festivals.



The Plakas: The Plakas is a cramped and squalid area located on the outskirts of Collytus. The houses are poorly constructed and cramped together, with narrow alleys separating them. The streets are littered with refuse and the stench of poverty and neglect fills the air. The residents of the Plakas are the poorest of the poor, struggling to make ends meet and facing constant hardship. Despite its grim appearance, the Plakas is also home to a strong sense of community, with neighbors coming together to help each other in times of need.
